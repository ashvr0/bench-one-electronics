// 01 — Foundations · Ch. 1

Static electricity

Before circuits, before current — electricity starts with a simple fact: matter is made of charges, and charges can be moved from one place to another.

Charge is a property of matter

Every atom has a nucleus of protons (positive) and neutrons (neutral), surrounded by electrons (negative). Normally an atom has equal protons and electrons, so it's neutral overall. Rub two different materials together — like a balloon on hair — and electrons physically transfer from one surface to the other. One object ends up with extra electrons (net negative charge), the other loses electrons (net positive charge). Nothing was created; charge just moved.

i

This is exactly what All About Circuits' Direct Current textbook opens with — static electricity is the simplest possible demonstration that charge is real and moveable, before any current is involved at all.

Like charges repel, opposite charges attract

This one rule explains static shocks, why your hair stands up after pulling off a wool hat, and why lightning happens. Rub the balloon to charge it, then slide it toward the wall — try it again with the wall charged the same way.

FAR CLOSE
Balloon: neutral. Slide it toward the wall — nothing happens yet.

Why this matters for circuits

A circuit is really just a controlled, continuous version of this same charge movement — instead of one static transfer, we set up a path where charge keeps flowing steadily. Everything from here on (current, voltage, resistance) is describing that flow in more precise terms.

Coulomb's law: how strong is the pull?

The force between two charges follows an inverse-square law — double the distance, and the force drops to a quarter, not half.

This is Coulomb's law: F = k × (q1 × q2) / r², where k is a constant, q1 and q2 are the charge amounts, and r is the distance between them. Two things make the force stronger: bigger charges, or a shorter distance — and distance matters far more, since it's squared.

Relative force

This is why static cling is strong right at contact and vanishes within an inch, and why a balloon "sticks" to a wall only when held close. This same inverse-square shape shows up again later for magnetic and gravitational fields — it's one of the most common patterns in physics.

Electrostatic discharge and real-world risk

Static buildup isn't just a curiosity — it's a real hazard for electronics.

A person walking across carpet can build up thousands of volts of static charge. Touch a sensitive chip, and that discharge (ESD, electrostatic discharge) can silently destroy it, even if you don't feel a shock. This is why technicians use anti-static wrist straps and ESD mats when handling bare circuit boards — they give any built-up charge a safe path to ground before it can jump through a component.

i

You can feel an ESD shock around 3,000V — but chips can be damaged by static as low as 100V, well below what you'd ever notice. This is why "I didn't feel anything" doesn't mean a board is safe from static damage.

The triboelectric series

Why some materials always seem to steal electrons from others, no matter what you rub them against.

Materials can be ranked by how strongly they tend to gain or lose electrons on contact — this ranking is called the triboelectric series. Materials near the top (like human hair, glass, wool) tend to give up electrons and charge positive. Materials near the bottom (like Teflon, vinyl) tend to grab electrons and charge negative. The farther apart two materials sit on the series, the stronger the charge transfer when they touch.

Conductors, insulators, and electron flow

Not every material lets charge move through it equally well. This single difference is why wires are made of copper and coated in plastic.

conductor

Conductors

Materials whose outer electrons are loosely held, so they move freely between atoms. Metals — copper, silver, gold, aluminum — are the best common conductors. This is why wires are metal.

Outer electrons drift freely between atoms

insulator

Insulators

Materials whose electrons are tightly bound to their atoms and don't move easily. Rubber, glass, plastic, and dry wood are common insulators — this is why wire is coated in plastic.

Electrons stay locked in orbit around their atom

Electron flow: a chain reaction, not a sprint

A useful mental model: imagine a tube completely packed end-to-end with marbles. Push one marble in at one end, and a marble pops out the other end almost instantly — not because that exact marble traveled the whole tube, but because the push transmits through the chain. Electrons in a wire behave similarly: the electric field pushes through the conductor near light-speed, even though any individual electron drifts quite slowly.

Semiconductors: the in-between material

Not everything is cleanly a conductor or an insulator. Semiconductors sit in the middle — and that middle ground is what makes every chip on Earth possible.

Silicon, the most common semiconductor, conducts far worse than copper but far better than glass. On its own it's not very useful — the real trick is doping: adding tiny, carefully controlled impurities that either add extra free electrons (n-type) or leave "holes" that behave like positive charge carriers (p-type). Combine n-type and p-type silicon and you get a diode, a transistor, and ultimately a whole processor.

Insulator Semiconductor Conductor

Glass: electrons locked tightly in place. Practically no conduction at room temperature.

This is why doping matters so much: it lets engineers dial a semiconductor's conductivity almost anywhere between insulator and conductor, on purpose, in specific regions of a chip — something you simply can't do with a fixed material like copper or rubber.

Superconductivity: zero resistance

Cool certain materials down far enough, and their electrical resistance doesn't just drop — it vanishes completely.

Below a material-specific critical temperature, some materials become superconductors: current can flow through them with zero energy loss, no voltage source required to keep it going. This isn't "very low resistance" — it's exactly zero, a genuinely different physical state. MRI machines, particle accelerators, and maglev trains all depend on this effect.

What are electric circuits?

A circuit is nothing more than a complete, unbroken loop that charge can flow around.

The three required ingredients

A source

Something that provides the "push" — a battery, a power supply, a solar cell. It maintains a voltage difference between its two terminals.

A path

A conductor (wire) connecting the source's terminals, so charge has somewhere to actually go.

A load

Something that uses the electrical energy — an LED, motor, resistor, or speaker — converting it into light, motion, or heat.

Open vs. closed: the loop has to be unbroken

A circuit only works when the loop is closed — a complete, unbroken path from one battery terminal, through the load, back to the other terminal. Break that loop anywhere (a switch, a disconnected wire, a burnt-out component) and you have an open circuit: no path, no current, nothing happens. Flip the switch below to see the difference.

Short circuits: when the load gets skipped

A closed loop is good. A closed loop with nothing to slow the current down is a problem.

A short circuit happens when current finds a path around the load entirely — straight from one battery terminal back to the other through little more than wire. Since Ohm's law says I = V/R, and a bare wire's resistance is nearly zero, the current spikes toward enormous values almost instantly. That's where the heat, sparks, and melted insulation in "don't touch the two battery terminals with a paperclip" warnings come from.

This is exactly why fuses and circuit breakers exist: they're designed to break the loop automatically once current crosses a safe threshold, before wires overheat or a battery is damaged.

// 04 — Foundations · Ch. 1

Voltage and current

Two ideas explain almost every circuit you'll ever look at. Here they are, using water as the analogy — because electricity is invisible, but water isn't.

Voltage (V)

The "pressure" pushing electricity through a wire — like water pressure in a pipe. Measured in volts. A 9V battery pushes harder than a 1.5V one.

Current (I)

The actual flow rate of electricity — how much water moves past a point per second. Measured in amps (A). Small electronics often use milliamps (mA).

Resistance (R)

How much something restricts that flow — a narrow pipe. Measured in ohms (Ω). Resistors are the component that adds this on purpose.

AC vs. DC — two ways current flows

DC — Direct Current

Flows in one direction only, at a steady level. This is what batteries, USB power, and most small electronics projects use — including everything you'll breadboard in class.

AC — Alternating Current

Flow direction switches back and forth many times a second. This is what comes out of a wall outlet, because it travels efficiently over long power-grid distances.

!

Never open or probe anything plugged directly into a wall outlet. All the projects in this guide use low-voltage DC (batteries, USB, or a benchtop supply under 12V), which is safe to touch.

The water analogy, taken further

Pressure, pipe width, and flow rate map onto voltage, resistance, and current almost perfectly. Adjust both and watch how flow actually responds.

The analogy holds because both systems obey the same shape of relationship: flow depends on push, divided by restriction. Increase the pressure (voltage) and flow (current) goes up. Widen the pipe — lower the resistance — and flow goes up too, for the same pressure. This is Ohm's law, wearing a plumbing costume.

One place the analogy breaks down: water can compress and pool, but in a simple DC circuit, current is the same at every point around the loop at any instant — there's no "backing up" the way water can pool behind a narrow point.

"Direct" and "alternating" describe a shape over time — seeing that shape makes the difference click instantly.

Plot voltage against time and DC draws a flat, steady line — always positive, never changing (ignoring brief startup transients). // 05 — Foundations · Ch. 1

Resistance, on its own

Resistance isn't just a resistor's job — every material has some, and it's the third piece Ohm's law depends on.

What determines a wire's resistance

Four factors, all intuitive once you see them:

Material — copper resists less than iron; some materials are just better conductors than others.

Length — a longer wire has more resistance. Twice the length, twice the resistance.

Cross-sectional area — a thicker wire has less resistance. Think of a wider pipe letting more water through for the same push.

Temperature — for most conductors, resistance rises as temperature rises.

Conductance: the flip side

Resistance's inverse is conductance (G = 1/R), measured in siemens (S). High resistance means low conductance and vice versa — same physical property, just described from the opposite direction. You'll see conductance far less often than resistance, but it shows up in parallel-circuit math later.

i

Superconductors are real materials that drop to essentially zero resistance at extremely low temperatures — current can flow through them without any voltage source maintaining it, in principle forever.

Temperature and resistance

Heat up a metal wire and its resistance climbs — a real effect with real consequences for anything that runs hot.

In most metals, heating the material makes atoms vibrate more, which gets in the way of moving electrons more often — resistance rises. This is why a light bulb's filament (which glows red-hot) has noticeably higher resistance running than it does cold, and why high-power electronics need cooling: rising resistance from heat can create a feedback loop of even more heat.

1.00×

This behavior is called a positive temperature coefficient. A few special materials do the opposite (resistance drops as they heat up) — those are called NTC (negative temperature coefficient) materials, and they're used deliberately in some sensors and inrush-current limiters.

Voltage and current in a practical circuit

Theory meets the real bench: what actually happens when you connect a battery, a switch, and an LED together.

Every real circuit needs a complete path — and something to limit current

Connect a battery directly to an LED with a bare wire and no resistor, and you've built a short circuit in slow motion: with almost no resistance in the path, Ohm's law (I = V / R) says the current spikes extremely high. That's exactly why an LED needs a series resistor: it adds enough resistance to keep the current in a safe range instead of burning out the LED (or the battery) instantly.

Voltage drop: where the "push" gets used up

As current flows through each component in a loop, the voltage supplied by the source gets "spent" across each part it passes through — this is called a voltage drop. In a simple series circuit with one resistor, the entire battery voltage drops across that one resistor. Add more components in series, and the total voltage divides across all of them, proportional to each one's resistance — you'll formalize this as voltage-divider math in a later chapter.

A worked example

9V battery → 470Ω resistor → red LED (needs ~2V, wants ~15mA) → back to battery.

Voltage left for the resistor to drop: 9V − 2V = 7V.

Current through the circuit: I = V / R = 7V / 470Ω ≈ 14.9mA — right in the LED's safe range. This is the exact calculation the Ohm's law calculator a few pages back does live.

Why every LED circuit needs a resistor, quantified

Working through what happens with zero resistance in the loop

Plug Ohm's law's numbers in: with a 9V battery, an LED that only needs ~2V, and essentially 0Ω of resistance in a bare-wire path, the "extra" 7V has nowhere sensible to go — the circuit tries to push an enormous current to satisfy I = V/R as R approaches zero. In practice, the LED (or the battery, or the wire) fails first, well before that theoretical current is ever reached.

!

This is exactly what "short circuit" means in practice: not necessarily a wire touching another wire, but any path with resistance too low for the available voltage — an LED with no series resistor qualifies just as much as two bare wires touching.

Voltage drop across multiple components

Extending the worked example to a series chain of more than one part

The main lesson's example uses one resistor and one LED. Add a second resistor in series, and the same principle just splits three ways instead of two: the battery's total voltage divides across all three components, in proportion to each one's resistance (or, for the LED, its fixed forward-voltage requirement), and the leftover always sums back up to the full supply voltage.

9V battery → 220Ω resistor → 250Ω resistor → LED (2V) → back to battery. Voltage left for the two resistors together: 9V − 2V = 7V. Current: I = 7V / (220Ω+250Ω) = 7V / 470Ω ≈ 14.9mA — identical math to the single-resistor case, just with resistances added first, exactly like the series rule from a later chapter.

Conventional versus electron flow

A historical accident that still shows up in every textbook and every schematic you'll read.

The mix-up, and why it stuck

In the 1700s, Benjamin Franklin guessed that charge flowed from positive to negative — and defined "positive" and "negative" based on that guess. It turns out he guessed the direction backwards: electrons (the things actually moving in a wire) are negatively charged and flow from the negative terminal to the positive terminal. But by the time this was discovered, the positive/negative naming convention and circuit diagrams were already everywhere.

the standard

Conventional current

Defined as flowing from + to − through the external circuit. This is what every schematic arrow, textbook diagram, and circuit law (including Kirchhoff's) assumes by default.

what physically moves

Electron flow

The actual electrons move from − to + through the external circuit — the literal opposite direction from the arrows on the diagram.

Arrows point + to − — the textbook direction

Same wire, same physics — two conventions pointing opposite ways.

!

This doesn't change any of the math. Whether you think of current as + → − or − → +, Ohm's law, circuit analysis, and every formula in this guide give identical answers — it's a labeling convention, not a physics disagreement. Stick with conventional current (+ → −) since that's what you'll see in class and in every datasheet.

Does the direction convention ever actually matter?

The rare cases where the choice of convention does show up

For almost everything in this guide — Ohm's law, series/parallel math, Kirchhoff's laws — the convention is purely cosmetic; the numbers come out identical either way. It becomes genuinely relevant in a few specific areas: semiconductor physics (where "electron flow" and "hole flow," a related but distinct concept, are both used to explain how transistors work), and in some magnetic-field right-hand-rule conventions, where getting the current direction backwards flips the predicted field direction.

For everyday circuit analysis and every calculator in this guide, conventional current (+ → −) is the only convention you need — the electron-flow detail is worth knowing exists, not worth switching to.

// 07 — Go deeper

Franklin's guess, and why it wasn't corrected

The historical reason the 'wrong' convention became permanent

By the time J.J. Thomson discovered the electron in 1897 — more than a century after Franklin's original guess — the positive/negative naming convention was already deeply embedded in published physics, early circuit theory, and emerging electrical engineering practice. Flipping the convention at that point would have meant rewriting an enormous body of existing work and re-teaching an already-standardized system, for a change that (as covered in the main lesson) doesn't affect any of the actual math.

So instead, the field kept Franklin's convention and simply added a footnote: current flows + to −, even though the electrons carrying it move the other way. That footnote is precisely what this chapter exists to explain.

Series vs. parallel circuits

Every circuit is built from just two wiring patterns, combined in different ways. Knowing which one you're looking at tells you how voltage and current behave.

Series

One path. Components are chained end-to-end, so the same current flows through all of them. If one breaks, the whole circuit stops — like old-style Christmas lights.

Parallel

Multiple paths. Each component connects across the same two points, so they all get the same voltage. If one breaks, the others keep working — like the outlets in a house.

Quick rules worth memorizing

SERIES

Current: same everywhere

Voltage: splits across components

Total resistance: adds up (R1+R2+...)

PARALLEL

Current: splits across branches

Voltage: same across every branch

Total resistance: always less than the smallest R

Equivalent resistance, one step further

Combining more than two resistors, and why parallel always drops below the smallest

The pattern from two resistors extends to any number: for a series chain, just keep adding — R_total = R1 + R2 + R3 + .... For a parallel group, the general formula is 1/R_total = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + .... Note that adding more parallel branches always makes total resistance go down, never up — you're giving current more paths, so overall it's easier for current to flow.

Three 100Ω resistors in parallel: 1/R = 1/100 + 1/100 + 1/100 = 3/100, so R = 33.3Ω — well below any single 100Ω resistor.

A useful shortcut: for n identical resistors of value R in parallel, the total is simply R / n.

// 10 — Go deeper

Why parallel outlets don't dim each other

The real-world reason your house wiring uses parallel, not series

Household outlets are wired in parallel specifically so every outlet sees the same ~120V (or 230V), independent of how many other things are plugged in nearby, and so one dead lamp doesn't kill the whole circuit. If outlets were wired in series like old Christmas lights, unplugging one appliance would cut power to everything downstream of it — and every device would have to share the same current.

!

This is also why adding more devices to one household circuit increases total current draw (parallel branches add current, not resistance) — enough devices on one breaker and you trip it, even though each device still sees full voltage.

Series-parallel combination circuits

Most real circuits aren't purely series or purely parallel — they're a mix. The trick is spotting which parts are which, then collapsing the circuit one step at a time.

The reduction strategy

Work from the outside in: find a small group of resistors that are purely series or purely parallel with each other, combine them into a single equivalent resistance, redraw the (now simpler) circuit, and repeat. Eventually the whole network reduces to one equivalent resistor.

R1 ∥ R2 is one parallel group; R3 ∥ R4 is another. Those two groups are in series with each other. Reduce each parallel pair to a single equivalent resistance, and you're left with a simple two-resistor series circuit.

Worked example

R1 = 100Ω, R2 = 100Ω (parallel) → R3 = 220Ω, R4 = 330Ω (parallel), the two groups in series, fed by a 9V source.

R1∥R2 = (100×100)/(100+100) = 50Ω

R3∥R4 = (220×330)/(220+330) = 132Ω

Total: 50Ω + 132Ω = 182Ω. Total current from the source: I = 9V / 182Ω ≈ 49.5mA — from here you can work backward to find the voltage and current at each original resistor.

!

Component failures are easier to reason about once you can see the circuit this way. An open resistor in a parallel group just removes that branch (the group's resistance rises); a short across a parallel group removes the whole group's resistance (drops to ~0Ω), which dumps extra voltage onto whatever's left in series with it.

Ladder networks: a common trap

Why some circuits look series-parallel but aren't — and how to spot the difference

Not every tangled-looking circuit reduces cleanly with series/parallel rules. A classic example is a ladder network or a bridge circuit, where a resistor connects two points that aren't a simple two-terminal group — there's no way to isolate a purely-series or purely-parallel chunk. These require more general tools like Kirchhoff's laws (next chapter) or nodal analysis, not just series/parallel reduction.

Quick test: if you can trace two clear endpoints for a group of resistors where current only enters and leaves at those two points, series/parallel reduction works. If a resistor bridges across the "middle" of the circuit, you likely need Kirchhoff's laws instead.

// 11 — Go deeper

Does reduction order matter?

Reassuringly, no — any valid reduction path gets you the same answer

If a circuit has multiple independent series/parallel groups, you can reduce them in whatever order you like — start with whichever group looks simplest. The final equivalent resistance comes out identical either way, because each reduction step is just applying algebra to a piece of the circuit, and algebra doesn't care what order you do it in.

This is genuinely useful on exams and on the bench: if a big combination circuit is intimidating, just find any resistor pair or chain that's clearly series or clearly parallel, collapse it, and repeat. Momentum matters more than a "correct" starting point.

Kirchhoff's laws

Two rules that let you analyze any circuit, no matter how tangled — they're really just "what goes in must come out" applied to current and voltage.

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

At any junction (node) in a circuit, the total current flowing in equals the total current flowing out. Charge can't pile up or vanish at a junction — it has to go somewhere.

6A comes in, so 6A must leave — split however the branch resistances demand (here, 4A + 2A). If you ever calculate currents at a junction that don't balance, you've made an arithmetic or wiring-direction mistake somewhere.

Formula: ΣI_in = ΣI_out

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

Around any complete closed loop in a circuit, the sum of all voltage rises equals the sum of all voltage drops — put another way, the voltages around a loop add up to zero. The battery's push has to be fully "spent" by the time you get back to where you started.

Battery: 9V. Resistor 1 drops 3V. Resistor 2 drops 6V.

9V = 3V + 6V — the loop balances. This is exactly the math behind series voltage dividers.

Kirchhoff's Current Law, visually

Why current in always equals current out at any junction

KCL says the sum of currents entering a junction (node) equals the sum of currents leaving it. This isn't a special electrical rule — it's just conservation of charge: charge can't pile up or vanish at a wire junction, so whatever flows in has to flow back out somewhere.

A node with 3A flowing in from one wire and 1A flowing in from another must have exactly 4A flowing out through whatever wires remain — split however the resistances downstream dictate, but always summing to 4A.

// 12 — Go deeper

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law, visually

Why voltage around any closed loop always sums to zero

KVL says that if you add up every voltage rise and every voltage drop as you trace a complete loop in a circuit, the total is zero. Think of it like elevation on a hike: if you start and end at the same point, all the uphill and downhill sections must cancel out — you can't end up net higher or lower than where you started.

In practice: a battery's voltage rise around a loop must exactly equal the sum of all the voltage drops across every resistor in that loop. This is the formal justification behind the voltage-divider shortcut from the next chapter.

When Ohm's law bends: non-linear components

Why LEDs, diodes, and lightbulb filaments don't follow a straight line

Ohm's law describes an ideal resistor, where R stays constant no matter what voltage you apply — plot V against I and you get a perfectly straight line. Real components like LEDs and diodes have resistance that changes with voltage and temperature, so their V-I curve bends instead. Below a certain "turn-on" voltage almost no current flows at all; past it, current rises steeply.

This is exactly why LEDs need a current-limiting series resistor rather than being wired straight to a battery — without something linear in the loop, a small voltage increase can cause a large, potentially destructive current spike.

Where the power actually goes: heat

Why resistors, wires, and chips get warm — and how that becomes a design constraint

In a plain resistor, essentially all dissipated power converts to heat — this is why resistors carry a power rating (like 1/4W) in addition to a resistance value. Power and efficiency in real designs

Why engineers obsess over wasted watts, from phone chargers to motors

Polarity: the mistake every beginner makes once

Which components care which way current flows, and why

Some components are polarized — they only work correctly (or safely) installed one direction. LEDs, electrolytic capacitors, and diodes are the classic offenders. Capacitors in AC circuits

Why a capacitor blocks DC but 'passes' AC

A capacitor never actually lets current cross the gap between its plates — but in an AC circuit, the voltage is constantly reversing, so the capacitor is perpetually charging and discharging in alternating directions. From outside the capacitor, that looks like current is flowing continuously, even though nothing crosses the dielectric. This "AC passes, DC blocks" behavior is the basis of using capacitors as filters — separating AC signal components from a steady DC level.

This links directly to the RC time constant from the main lesson: at low frequencies (slow voltage changes) a capacitor behaves more like it's blocking; at high frequencies it behaves more like it's passing current through.

How this becomes a motor

From a current-carrying wire in a field to continuous rotation

A wire carrying current inside a magnetic field feels a force (this is the same physics as the electromagnet you just read about, run in reverse). Shape that wire into a loop mounted on an axle, keep it inside a fixed magnet's field, and that force becomes a torque that spins the loop. A commutator flips the current's direction at just the right moment each half-turn so the torque keeps pushing the same rotational direction instead of oscillating back and forth — and you have a basic DC motor.

A speaker uses the same underlying principle in reverse-ish form: an audio signal's changing current pushes a coil (attached to a cone) back and forth inside a fixed magnet, and that physical motion is what you hear as sound.

Inductors and the fight against current change

Why inductors resist sudden change, and why that matters for switching circuits

An inductor is just a coil of wire, and it stores energy in its magnetic field rather than in an electric field like a capacitor does. Any change in current through an inductor changes its magnetic field, which in turn generates a voltage (called back-EMF) that opposes that change — inductors resist sudden current changes the way capacitors resist sudden voltage changes.

This matters practically: suddenly switching off current to an inductive load (like a motor or relay coil) can generate a large voltage spike as the field collapses — which is why such circuits often include a "flyback diode" to safely absorb that spike instead of letting it damage nearby components.

Soldering basics

Soldering permanently joins components to a circuit using melted metal alloy. It looks intimidating; it isn't, once you've done it once correctly.

!

The iron tip reaches 300–400°C (570–750°F). Always rest it in its stand when not actively soldering, work in a ventilated area, and keep it away from cords, skin, and anything flammable.

What's in your toolkit

Soldering iron — the heated tool that melts solder onto joints.

Solder — the thin wire of metal alloy that melts and forms the joint.

Helping hands / vice — holds your board steady so both hands are free.

The process

  1. 1

    Heat the joint, not the solder

    Touch the iron tip to both the component lead and the pad at once for 1–2 seconds. This heats the metal you're joining, not just the solder itself.

  2. 2

    Feed solder into the joint

    Touch the solder wire to the heated joint (not the iron tip directly). It should melt and flow smoothly around the connection.

  3. 3

    Remove solder, then iron

    Pull the solder wire away first, then the iron a moment later. This gives the joint time to form cleanly.

  4. 4

    Let it cool, don't blow on it

    Hold the joint still for a few seconds while it solidifies. Moving it while still molten creates a weak "cold joint."

What a good joint looks like

Shiny, smooth, and shaped like a small volcano cone around the lead — not a dull grey blob (cold joint) and not a giant ball (too much solder).

Everything past this point in the guide runs on two voltage levels: high and low. Before gates, before code, you need to think the way the hardware does — in bits.

Why two levels, not ten

Converting between binary and decimal by hand

Building a half-adder

Here's where gates stop being a toy and start being a computer. Combine an XOR and an AND gate, and you get a circuit that adds two single bits — the literal building block of every calculation a processor ever performs.

The problem: add two bits

From half-adder to full-adder

The extra input that makes multi-bit addition actually work

A half-adder handles one bit-pair but has no way to accept a carry-in from a previous column — which means it can't be chained to add multi-bit numbers on its own. Data types: why int, float, and bool aren't interchangeable

What's actually happening in memory when you pick a variable type

C++ is a statically typed language — every variable's type is fixed at compile time and determines exactly how many bytes of memory it uses and how those bytes are interpreted. Variable scope: why your sketch behaves differently inside vs. outside loop()

Digital I/O, pull-ups, and debouncing

Reading a button seems trivial — until your circuit reports six presses for one tap. This is the most common "my code is broken" bug in embedded electronics, and it isn't a code bug at all.

pinMode has three options, not two

OUTPUT — the pin actively drives HIGH or LOW. Used for LEDs, motors, anything you're controlling.

INPUT — the pin reads voltage, but is left "floating" if nothing's connected — it can pick up electrical noise and read randomly.

INPUT_PULLUP — the pin reads voltage, with a built-in resistor quietly holding it HIGH until something pulls it LOW. This is what you want for a button.

With INPUT_PULLUP, wiring gets simpler too: connect the button between the pin and ground — no external resistor needed. The pin reads HIGH normally and LOW when pressed (backwards from what you might expect at first).

The problem: switch bounce

A mechanical switch doesn't cleanly transition from open to closed. The metal contacts physically bounce for a few milliseconds, generating a rapid on-off-on-off flicker before settling. Your microcontroller reads fast enough to see every one of those bounces as a separate press.

The fix, in code

Ignore any change that happens too soon after the last one — enforce a minimum time gap using millis(), Arduino's running clock in milliseconds since power-on.

const int buttonPin = 2;
unsigned long lastPressTime = 0;
const unsigned long DEBOUNCE_MS = 50; // bounce settles well within 50ms
 
void setup() {
  pinMode(buttonPin, INPUT_PULLUP);
  Serial.begin(9600);
}
 
void loop() {
  if (digitalRead(buttonPin) == LOW) {
    if (millis() - lastPressTime > DEBOUNCE_MS) {
      Serial.println("Button pressed");
      lastPressTime = millis();
    }
  }
}

Internal pull-ups: skipping the extra resistor

Debouncing in code vs. debouncing in hardware

Two different fixes for the same mechanical bounce problem

The main lesson covers the problem — a mechanical switch's contacts physically bounce for a few milliseconds, registering as several rapid presses instead of one. How shift registers 'speak' to the Arduino

The three signals (data, clock, latch) that drive a 74HC595

A shift register like the 74HC595 uses just three pins from the Arduino: data (the bit value to shift in), clock (a pulse that shifts that bit into the register), and latch (a pulse that copies the shifted-in bits to the output pins all at once). This 3-wire pattern (data, clock, latch) is a simplified relative of SPI, a communication protocol used constantly for talking to displays, sensors, and memory chips — learning it here pays off well beyond just driving LEDs.

PWM vs. a true analog output (DAC)

Why PWM is a clever workaround, not the same thing as real analog voltage

PWM never actually outputs a voltage between HIGH and LOW — it only ever outputs full HIGH or full LOW, rapidly. What makes it useful is that downstream devices (an LED, your eye, a motor's mechanical inertia) effectively "average" that rapid switching into something that behaves like an intermediate value. A true DAC (digital-to-analog converter) outputs a genuinely continuous voltage instead — more accurate for some applications (like audio), but requiring dedicated hardware most basic microcontrollers don't include on every pin.

For LED brightness and simple motor speed control, PWM's "fake it with fast switching" approach is close enough that a real DAC is rarely worth the added complexity.

The rules for writing a safe ISR

Why interrupt service routines need to be short and careful

An interrupt service routine (ISR) should do as little as possible — ideally just set a flag or update a variable — because it runs at the expense of whatever the main loop() was doing, and other interrupts are typically blocked while it executes. Interrupts vs. polling: picking the right tool

When polling is actually the better, simpler choice

A good rule of thumb: reach for interrupts when timing precision genuinely matters and polling would risk missing the event; otherwise, plain polling is usually the more maintainable choice.

The states, and the rule between them

The full sketch

enum LightState { RED, GREEN, YELLOW };
LightState currentState = RED;
unsigned long stateStartTime = 0;
 
const unsigned long RED_MS = 4000;
const unsigned long GREEN_MS = 3000;
const unsigned long YELLOW_MS = 1000;
 
void setup() {
  pinMode(REDPIN, OUTPUT); pinMode(YELLOWPIN, OUTPUT); pinMode(GREENPIN, OUTPUT);
  stateStartTime = millis();
}
 
void loop() {
  unsigned long elapsed = millis() - stateStartTime;
 
  switch (currentState) {
    case RED:
      if (elapsed >= RED_MS) { currentState = GREEN; stateStartTime = millis(); }
      break;
    case GREEN:
      if (elapsed >= GREEN_MS) { currentState = YELLOW; stateStartTime = millis(); }
      break;
    case YELLOW:
      if (elapsed >= YELLOW_MS) { currentState = RED; stateStartTime = millis(); }
      break;
  }
 
  digitalWrite(REDPIN, currentState == RED);
  digitalWrite(YELLOWPIN, currentState == YELLOW);
  digitalWrite(GREENPIN, currentState == GREEN);
}

Why state machines beat a pile of if-statements

The maintainability problem FSMs solve as projects grow

It's entirely possible to build a traffic light using a tangle of booleans and nested if statements — but as the number of states and transitions grows, that approach becomes fragile fast: it's easy to create impossible combinations (like two lights green at once) or forget a transition. A state machine forces you to explicitly enumerate every valid state and every valid transition between them up front, which makes invalid combinations structurally impossible rather than something you have to remember to prevent.

This same pattern — enumerated states plus explicit transitions — shows up everywhere in software engineering, from game character behavior to network protocol implementations, well beyond hobby electronics.

Where state machines show up next

How this capstone pattern generalizes to bigger embedded projects

Once you're comfortable with a simple traffic-light FSM, the same skeleton scales to far more complex embedded projects: a game console's menu system, a washing machine's wash cycle, a vending machine's transaction flow, or a robot's behavior tree are all built from the same core idea — a current state, a set of allowed transitions, and conditions that trigger each transition.

If you want to keep pushing this further, look into hierarchical state machines (states that contain their own sub-states) — a natural next step once a flat state machine's transition list starts getting unwieldy.
