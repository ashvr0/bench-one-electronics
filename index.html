Static electricity

Before circuits, before current — electricity starts with a simple fact: matter is made of charges, and charges can be moved from one place to another.

Charge is a property of matter

Every atom has a nucleus of protons (positive) and neutrons (neutral), surrounded by electrons (negative). Normally an atom has equal protons and electrons, so it's neutral overall. Rub two different materials together — like a balloon on hair — and electrons physically transfer from one surface to the other. One object ends up with extra electrons (net negative charge), the other loses electrons (net positive charge). Nothing was created; charge just moved.

i

This is exactly what All About Circuits' Direct Current textbook opens with — static electricity is the simplest possible demonstration that charge is real and moveable, before any current is involved at all.

Like charges repel, opposite charges attract

This one rule explains static shocks, why your hair stands up after pulling off a wool hat, and why lightning happens. Rub the balloon to charge it, then slide it toward the wall — try it again with the wall charged the same way.

FAR CLOSE
Balloon: neutral. Slide it toward the wall — nothing happens yet.

Why this matters for circuits

A circuit is really just a controlled, continuous version of this same charge movement — instead of one static transfer, we set up a path where charge keeps flowing steadily. Everything from here on (current, voltage, resistance) is describing that flow in more precise terms.

Conductors, insulators, and electron flow

Not every material lets charge move through it equally well. This single difference is why wires are made of copper and coated in plastic.

conductor

Conductors

Materials whose outer electrons are loosely held, so they move freely between atoms. Metals — copper, silver, gold, aluminum — are the best common conductors. This is why wires are metal.

Outer electrons drift freely between atoms

insulator

Insulators

Materials whose electrons are tightly bound to their atoms and don't move easily. Rubber, glass, plastic, and dry wood are common insulators — this is why wire is coated in plastic.

Electrons stay locked in orbit around their atom

Electron flow: a chain reaction, not a sprint

A useful mental model: imagine a tube completely packed end-to-end with marbles. Push one marble in at one end, and a marble pops out the other end almost instantly — not because that exact marble traveled the whole tube, but because the push transmits through the chain. Electrons in a wire behave similarly: the electric field pushes through the conductor near light-speed, even though any individual electron drifts quite slowly.

Toggle the switch to push a new electron in at the left — watch one pop out the right almost immediately, just like the marble tube.

What are electric circuits?

A circuit is nothing more than a complete, unbroken loop that charge can flow around.

The three required ingredients

A source

Something that provides the "push" — a battery, a power supply, a solar cell. It maintains a voltage difference between its two terminals.

A path

A conductor (wire) connecting the source's terminals, so charge has somewhere to actually go.

A load

Something that uses the electrical energy — an LED, motor, resistor, or speaker — converting it into light, motion, or heat.

Open vs. closed: the loop has to be unbroken

A circuit only works when the loop is closed — a complete, unbroken path from one battery terminal, through the load, back to the other terminal. Break that loop anywhere (a switch, a disconnected wire, a burnt-out component) and you have an open circuit: no path, no current, nothing happens. Flip the switch below to see the difference.

Voltage and current

Two ideas explain almost every circuit you'll ever look at. Here they are, using water as the analogy — because electricity is invisible, but water isn't.

Voltage (V)

The "pressure" pushing electricity through a wire — like water pressure in a pipe. Measured in volts. A 9V battery pushes harder than a 1.5V one.

Current (I)

The actual flow rate of electricity — how much water moves past a point per second. Measured in amps (A). Small electronics often use milliamps (mA).

Resistance (R)

How much something restricts that flow — a narrow pipe. Measured in ohms (Ω). Resistors are the component that adds this on purpose.

AC vs. DC — two ways current flows

DC — Direct Current

Flows in one direction only, at a steady level. This is what batteries, USB power, and most small electronics projects use — including everything you'll breadboard in class.

AC — Alternating Current

Flow direction switches back and forth many times a second. This is what comes out of a wall outlet, because it travels efficiently over long power-grid distances.

!

Never open or probe anything plugged directly into a wall outlet. All the projects in this guide use low-voltage DC (batteries, USB, or a benchtop supply under 12V), which is safe to touch.

Resistance, on its own

Resistance isn't just a resistor's job — every material has some, and it's the third piece Ohm's law depends on.

What determines a wire's resistance

Four factors, all intuitive once you see them:

Material — copper resists less than iron; some materials are just better conductors than others.

Length — a longer wire has more resistance. Twice the length, twice the resistance.

Cross-sectional area — a thicker wire has less resistance. Think of a wider pipe letting more water through for the same push.

Temperature — for most conductors, resistance rises as temperature rises.

Conductance: the flip side

Resistance's inverse is conductance (G = 1/R), measured in siemens (S). High resistance means low conductance and vice versa — same physical property, just described from the opposite direction. You'll see conductance far less often than resistance, but it shows up in parallel-circuit math later.

i

Superconductors are real materials that drop to essentially zero resistance at extremely low temperatures — current can flow through them without any voltage source maintaining it, in principle forever.

Voltage and current in a practical circuit

Theory meets the real bench: what actually happens when you connect a battery, a switch, and an LED together.

Every real circuit needs a complete path — and something to limit current

Connect a battery directly to an LED with a bare wire and no resistor, and you've built a short circuit in slow motion: with almost no resistance in the path, Ohm's law (I = V / R) says the current spikes extremely high. That's exactly why an LED needs a series resistor: it adds enough resistance to keep the current in a safe range instead of burning out the LED (or the battery) instantly.

Voltage drop: where the "push" gets used up

As current flows through each component in a loop, the voltage supplied by the source gets "spent" across each part it passes through — this is called a voltage drop. In a simple series circuit with one resistor, the entire battery voltage drops across that one resistor. Add more components in series, and the total voltage divides across all of them, proportional to each one's resistance — you'll formalize this as voltage-divider math in a later chapter.

A worked example

9V battery → 470Ω resistor → red LED (needs ~2V, wants ~15mA) → back to battery.

Voltage left for the resistor to drop: 9V − 2V = 7V.

Current through the circuit: I = V / R = 7V / 470Ω ≈ 14.9mA — right in the LED's safe range. This is the exact calculation the Ohm's law calculator a few pages back does live.

Conventional versus electron flow

A historical accident that still shows up in every textbook and every schematic you'll read.

The mix-up, and why it stuck

In the 1700s, Benjamin Franklin guessed that charge flowed from positive to negative — and defined "positive" and "negative" based on that guess. It turns out he guessed the direction backwards: electrons (the things actually moving in a wire) are negatively charged and flow from the negative terminal to the positive terminal. But by the time this was discovered, the positive/negative naming convention and circuit diagrams were already everywhere.

the standard

Conventional current

Defined as flowing from + to − through the external circuit. This is what every schematic arrow, textbook diagram, and circuit law (including Kirchhoff's) assumes by default.

what physically moves

Electron flow

The actual electrons move from − to + through the external circuit — the literal opposite direction from the arrows on the diagram.

Conventional current · + → −

Arrows point + to − — the textbook direction

Electron flow · − → +

Electrons actually travel − to + — the reverse

Same wire, same physics — two conventions pointing opposite ways.

!

This doesn't change any of the math. Whether you think of current as + → − or − → +, Ohm's law, circuit analysis, and every formula in this guide give identical answers — it's a labeling convention, not a physics disagreement. Stick with conventional current (+ → −) since that's what you'll see in class and in every datasheet.

Series vs. parallel circuits

Every circuit is built from just two wiring patterns, combined in different ways. Knowing which one you're looking at tells you how voltage and current behave.

Series

One path. Components are chained end-to-end, so the same current flows through all of them. If one breaks, the whole circuit stops — like old-style Christmas lights.

Parallel

Multiple paths. Each component connects across the same two points, so they all get the same voltage. If one breaks, the others keep working — like the outlets in a house.

Quick rules worth memorizing

SERIES

Current: same everywhere

Voltage: splits across components

Total resistance: adds up (R1+R2+...)

PARALLEL

Current: splits across branches

Voltage: same across every branch

Total resistance: always less than the smallest R

The table method

Whenever you're solving a series or parallel circuit, a simple table keeps E (voltage), I (current), and R organized by component — fill in whatever you're given, apply Ohm's law across each row, then use the series/parallel rule that applies down each column.

Series-parallel combination circuits

Most real circuits aren't purely series or purely parallel — they're a mix. The trick is spotting which parts are which, then collapsing the circuit one step at a time.

The reduction strategy

Work from the outside in: find a small group of resistors that are purely series or purely parallel with each other, combine them into a single equivalent resistance, redraw the (now simpler) circuit, and repeat. Eventually the whole network reduces to one equivalent resistor.

R1 ∥ R2 is one parallel group; R3 ∥ R4 is another. Those two groups are in series with each other. Reduce each parallel pair to a single equivalent resistance, and you're left with a simple two-resistor series circuit.

Worked example

R1 = 100Ω, R2 = 100Ω (parallel) → R3 = 220Ω, R4 = 330Ω (parallel), the two groups in series, fed by a 9V source.

R1∥R2 = (100×100)/(100+100) = 50Ω

R3∥R4 = (220×330)/(220+330) = 132Ω

Total: 50Ω + 132Ω = 182Ω. Total current from the source: I = 9V / 182Ω ≈ 49.5mA — from here you can work backward to find the voltage and current at each original resistor.

!

Component failures are easier to reason about once you can see the circuit this way. An open resistor in a parallel group just removes that branch (the group's resistance rises); a short across a parallel group removes the whole group's resistance (drops to ~0Ω), which dumps extra voltage onto whatever's left in series with it.

Kirchhoff's laws

Two rules that let you analyze any circuit, no matter how tangled — they're really just "what goes in must come out" applied to current and voltage.

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

At any junction (node) in a circuit, the total current flowing in equals the total current flowing out. Charge can't pile up or vanish at a junction — it has to go somewhere.

6A comes in, so 6A must leave — split however the branch resistances demand (here, 4A + 2A). If you ever calculate currents at a junction that don't balance, you've made an arithmetic or wiring-direction mistake somewhere.

Formula: ΣI_in = ΣI_out

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

Around any complete closed loop in a circuit, the sum of all voltage rises equals the sum of all voltage drops — put another way, the voltages around a loop add up to zero. The battery's push has to be fully "spent" by the time you get back to where you started.

Battery: 9V. Resistor 1 drops 3V. Resistor 2 drops 6V.

9V = 3V + 6V — the loop balances. This is exactly the math behind series voltage dividers.

Voltage and current divider circuits

Two of the most-used shortcuts in electronics — formulas that skip straight to "how much of the total ends up here" without solving the whole circuit first.

Voltage divider (series resistors)

In a series chain, each resistor gets a share of the total voltage proportional to its own resistance relative to the total. This is exactly what Kirchhoff's Voltage Law guarantees — the formula just skips ahead to the answer.

Vx = Vtotal × (Rx / Rtotal)

A voltage divider only outputs a clean, predictable voltage if very little current is drawn from the tap — connecting a low-resistance load there pulls the output down. This is why real designs often use a regulator instead of a plain divider when the output needs to drive something power-hungry.

Current divider (parallel resistors)

In a parallel pair, current splits between the branches — but in inverse proportion to resistance: more current takes the path of least resistance.

I1 = Itotal × (R2 / (R1 + R2))

Notice R2 (the other branch's resistance) is on top — the branch with less resistance gets more of the current.

Electrical safety

Low-voltage hobby electronics is genuinely safe — but knowing why, and where the real risks are, matters before you touch anything with a plug on it.

!

Current, not voltage, is what injures. A static shock can be thousands of volts but harmless because almost no current flows. Wall power at "only" 120V/230V can be lethal because it can drive dangerous current through your body.

Why your breadboard projects are safe

A 9V battery or USB power supply simply can't push enough current through your skin's resistance to cause harm — your body's resistance (thousands of ohms, dry skin) combined with Ohm's law keeps the current microscopically small. This is why every project in this guide uses low-voltage DC.

Rules that matter once you go beyond the breadboard

Never work on anything live. Unplug and verify with a multimeter before touching wall-power circuits.

One hand rule. Keep one hand in your pocket when probing higher-voltage circuits — it prevents current from having a path across your chest.

Capacitors can store a charge even after power is removed. Some can still shock you minutes or hours later.

Dry hands, dry surroundings. Wet skin has dramatically lower resistance, which allows dangerously higher current for the same voltage.

Power in electric circuits

Voltage and current tell you about the "push" and the "flow" — power tells you the actual rate of energy being used or delivered, in watts.

What power actually measures

Power (P) is the rate at which energy is transferred — how fast work gets done. One watt is one joule of energy per second. A component might see 12V and only 10mA, or 1V and 10A — very different power depending on the combination, even with similar-looking numbers.

P = I × V

Combine with Ohm's law (V = IR) to get two more useful forms: P = I²R and P = V²/R.

Meters: how they work and how to use them

A multimeter is really three (or four) different instruments in one case, each wired into the circuit differently. Knowing which mode does what — and how to connect it — prevents blown fuses and bad readings.

series

Ammeter

Measures current. Must be inserted in series — break the circuit and put the meter in the gap, so all the current is forced through it. Wiring it in parallel by mistake creates a near-short and can blow the meter's fuse.

parallel

Voltmeter

Measures voltage difference between two points. Connected in parallel, across the component — the circuit stays intact and the meter just "reads" the drop from the outside.

power off

Ohmmeter

Measures resistance by pushing its own small known current through the component and reading the voltage. Only ever use it on an unpowered circuit — testing resistance on a live circuit gives a meaningless reading and can damage the meter.

Why meter design changes the circuit slightly

No meter is perfectly invisible to the circuit it's measuring. An ideal ammeter would have zero resistance (so it doesn't add any voltage drop of its own); real ones have a small amount. An ideal voltmeter would have infinite resistance (so it draws zero current from the circuit it's reading); real ones draw a tiny amount. Cheap meters or very sensitive circuits are where this "loading effect" becomes noticeable — the act of measuring slightly disturbs what you're measuring.

Ammeter: in the current's path, in series

Voltmeter: across the load, in parallel

Capacitors

If a resistor is a narrow pipe, a capacitor is a small water tank plumbed into the circuit — it fills up, and it drains back out.

How they work

A capacitor is two conductive plates separated by an insulator (the dielectric). Apply voltage, and charge builds up on the plates — no current actually crosses the gap, but the buildup behaves like current flowing while it's charging. Measured in farads (F); since a farad is huge, you'll almost always see µF, nF, or pF.

Charge and discharge — the RC time constant

Charging isn't instant — it follows a curve. The time constant τ = R × C tells you how fast: after 1τ, the capacitor reaches ~63% of full charge; after 5τ, it's essentially fully charged (~99%). Flip the switch below to watch it charge and discharge through a resistor.

Formula: τ = R × C. Bigger R or C means a slower charge/discharge.

What capacitors are used for

Smoothing — filling in the gaps in a rectified AC signal to give steadier DC.

Decoupling — a small local charge reserve so a chip doesn't starve during current spikes.

Timing — combined with a resistor, sets delays (like a classic 555-timer blink circuit).

Magnetism, electromagnetism, and inductors

Moving charge and magnetism are two sides of the same phenomenon — this link is what makes motors, speakers, and inductors work.

Current creates a magnetic field

Any wire carrying current generates a magnetic field around it (this is electromagnetism). Coil that wire into a loop, and the fields from each turn add together — more turns, or more current, means a stronger field. This is literally how an electromagnet works: wire wrapped around an iron core, energized by current.

CURRENT 0%

Drag the slider up — more current, more visible field lines and a stronger pull.

Inductors: the electrical inertia component

An inductor is just a coil of wire. Its defining behavior is opposing changes in current — not blocking current itself, like a resistor, but resisting a sudden change in it, storing energy in its magnetic field while current rises and releasing it while current falls. Measured in henries (H).

Resistor: opposes current itself, in both DC and AC, no time-dependence.

Inductor: opposes changes in current — behaves almost like a wire to steady DC, but pushes back hard against sudden changes or fast AC.

This "resists change" behavior is why inductors show up in power supply filtering, motor circuits, and anywhere you need to smooth out sudden current spikes.

Microcontrollers: Arduino & Raspberry Pi

The two boards you'll hear about most in class — and they're not the same kind of thing.

microcontroller

Arduino

Runs one program at a time, directly on the chip, with no operating system. Great for reading sensors and controlling motors/LEDs with precise timing. Programmed in a simplified C++ from a computer, then runs standalone.

microcomputer

Raspberry Pi

A full tiny computer running Linux. Can multitask, browse the web, run Python scripts, and connect to a monitor/keyboard. Better for projects that need real computing power, like image processing.

Your first Arduino program: blink an LED

This is the "hello world" of electronics. Here's what the code does, line by line:

// runs once when the board powers on
void setup() {
  pinMode(13, OUTPUT); // pin 13 will send power out
}
 
// runs over and over, forever
void loop() {
  digitalWrite(13, HIGH); // turn LED on
  delay(1000); // wait 1 second
  digitalWrite(13, LOW); // turn LED off
  delay(1000); // wait 1 second
}

setup() runs once. loop() repeats forever — that's the pattern behind almost every Arduino sketch you'll write.

Soldering basics

Soldering permanently joins components to a circuit using melted metal alloy. It looks intimidating; it isn't, once you've done it once correctly.

!

The iron tip reaches 300–400°C (570–750°F). Always rest it in its stand when not actively soldering, work in a ventilated area, and keep it away from cords, skin, and anything flammable.

What's in your toolkit

Soldering iron — the heated tool that melts solder onto joints.

Solder — the thin wire of metal alloy that melts and forms the joint.

Helping hands / vice — holds your board steady so both hands are free.

The process

  1. 1

    Heat the joint, not the solder

    Touch the iron tip to both the component lead and the pad at once for 1–2 seconds. This heats the metal you're joining, not just the solder itself.

  2. 2

    Feed solder into the joint

    Touch the solder wire to the heated joint (not the iron tip directly). It should melt and flow smoothly around the connection.

  3. 3

    Remove solder, then iron

    Pull the solder wire away first, then the iron a moment later. This gives the joint time to form cleanly.

  4. 4

    Let it cool, don't blow on it

    Hold the joint still for a few seconds while it solidifies. Moving it while still molten creates a weak "cold joint."

What a good joint looks like

Shiny, smooth, and shaped like a small volcano cone around the lead — not a dull grey blob (cold joint) and not a giant ball (too much solder).

Troubleshooting a dead circuit

Almost every "it doesn't work" moment follows the same diagnostic path. Work through it top to bottom before assuming a part is broken.

Is power actually reaching the board?

Check with a multimeter, or a known-working LED, right at the power source.

NO

Check the battery/power supply, the power switch, and the cable. This alone fixes a huge share of "broken" circuits.

YES

Good — power's live. Move to checking the connections.

Are all connections solid?

Loose breadboard wires and cold solder joints cause more failures than bad components.

NO

Re-seat every wire firmly. On a breadboard, check you're in the correct connected rows/columns.

YES

Good — connections are solid. Move to checking orientation.

Are polarized parts oriented correctly?

LEDs, diodes, electrolytic capacitors, and ICs all care which way they face.

NO

Flip the part around. For an LED, the longer leg goes to positive (+).

YES

Good. Now isolate — test one section of the circuit at a time.

Still stuck? Divide and conquer.

Disconnect components one at a time and retest. If a working sub-circuit stops working when you add one part, that part (or its wiring) is the likely culprit.

Reading a multimeter

Voltage (V) — probes placed across (in parallel with) a component. No circuit break needed.

Continuity — beeps if there's a complete path. Great for checking wires and fuses.

Resistance (Ω) — only measure this with power disconnected from the circuit.

Binary & bits

Everything past this point in the guide runs on two voltage levels: high and low. Before gates, before code, you need to think the way the hardware does — in bits.

Why two levels, not ten

A wire is either carrying enough voltage to count as "on" (roughly 5V or 3.3V, depending on the board) or not (near 0V). Building reliable circuits that distinguish ten different voltage levels is hard and noise-prone; distinguishing two is easy and robust. That two-level system is binary — base 2, using only the digits 0 and 1, where each digit is called a bit.

Reading a binary number

Decimal place values are powers of 10 (1, 10, 100...). Binary place values are powers of 2 (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128...). To read a binary number, add up the place values where there's a 1.

Example: 1011 in binary = (1×8) + (0×4) + (1×2) + (1×1) = 8 + 0 + 2 + 1 = 11 in decimal.

Logic gates

A logic gate is a tiny circuit that takes one or two binary inputs and produces one binary output, based on a fixed rule. Every microcontroller, and every computer ever built, is these gates wired together at massive scale.

The six gates, at a glance

AND — output is 1 only if every input is 1. Think: both switches must be on.

OR — output is 1 if any input is 1.

NOT — one input, flips it. The only single-input gate here.

XOR — 1 only if the inputs differ. Used to detect change.

NAND — AND, then inverted. Famous because you can build any other gate from just NANDs.

NOR — OR, then inverted.

Building a half-adder

Here's where gates stop being a toy and start being a computer. Combine an XOR and an AND gate, and you get a circuit that adds two single bits — the literal building block of every calculation a processor ever performs.

The problem: add two bits

Adding two 1-bit numbers can produce a 2-bit result: 1 + 1 = 10 in binary (that's 2 in decimal). So the circuit needs two outputs: a Sum bit and a Carry bit.

Sum — is 1 when exactly one input is 1, but not both. That's exactly what XOR does.

Carry — is 1 only when both inputs are 1 (the case that overflows into the next place value). That's exactly what AND does.

Try it: flip the inputs

This is called a half-adder because it can't accept a carry-in from a previous column. Chain several together with an extra OR gate per stage (making each a "full adder") and you can add numbers of any width — which is precisely what happens inside a CPU's arithmetic logic unit.

Real C++ for Arduino

The blink sketch back in chapter 17 was "hello world." This is the actual language underneath it — variables, types, and functions, the way you'll really use them.

Variables have types, and types matter

Unlike some beginner-friendly languages, C++ makes you declare what kind of data a variable holds, and that choice affects both memory usage and behavior — critical on a chip with only a few kilobytes of RAM.

// each of these reserves a different amount of memory

unsigned long lastTime = 0; // big non-negative numbers — used for millis() timestamps

Functions: named, reusable blocks

You've already used two functions Arduino requires — setup() and loop() — but you can write your own. A function has a return type (what kind of value it hands back, or void for nothing), a name, and parameters in parentheses.

  return (celsius * 9 / 5) + 32;
}

Digital I/O, pull-ups, and debouncing

Reading a button seems trivial — until your circuit reports six presses for one tap. This is the most common "my code is broken" bug in embedded electronics, and it isn't a code bug at all.

pinMode has three options, not two

OUTPUT — the pin actively drives HIGH or LOW. Used for LEDs, motors, anything you're controlling.

INPUT — the pin reads voltage, but is left "floating" if nothing's connected — it can pick up electrical noise and read randomly.

INPUT_PULLUP — the pin reads voltage, with a built-in resistor quietly holding it HIGH until something pulls it LOW. This is what you want for a button.

With INPUT_PULLUP, wiring gets simpler too: connect the button between the pin and ground — no external resistor needed. The pin reads HIGH normally and LOW when pressed (backwards from what you might expect at first).

The problem: switch bounce

A mechanical switch doesn't cleanly transition from open to closed. The metal contacts physically bounce for a few milliseconds, generating a rapid on-off-on-off flicker before settling. Your microcontroller reads fast enough to see every one of those bounces as a separate press.

The fix, in code

Ignore any change that happens too soon after the last one — enforce a minimum time gap using millis(), Arduino's running clock in milliseconds since power-on.

const int buttonPin = 2;
unsigned long lastPressTime = 0;
const unsigned long DEBOUNCE_MS = 50; // bounce settles well within 50ms
 
void setup() {
  pinMode(buttonPin, INPUT_PULLUP);
  Serial.begin(9600);
}
 
void loop() {
  if (digitalRead(buttonPin) == LOW) {
    if (millis() - lastPressTime > DEBOUNCE_MS) {
      Serial.println("Button pressed");
      lastPressTime = millis();
    }
  }
}
!

Notice this uses millis(), not delay(). A delay() call freezes the entire chip — no sensors, no other logic — which is fine for a blink demo but breaks any real project doing more than one thing at once.

Shift registers

An Arduino Uno has about 14 usable digital pins. What happens when a project needs to control 20 LEDs? You stop wiring pins one-to-one and start shifting bits.

The idea: serial in, parallel out

A shift register (the classic chip is the 74HC595) takes bits in one at a time on a single data pin, and once all its internal slots are filled, it presents them simultaneously on multiple output pins. Feed it 8 bits serially, get 8 outputs in parallel — from just 3 control pins on the Arduino, no matter how many registers you chain together.

Try it: shift a byte in

Click "Shift in a 1" or "Shift in a 0" to push a new bit — watch it enter on the right and push everything else left, exactly like a line of people shuffling down a bench.

Register empty — 0b00000000

The three pins you wire it with

Data — the bit value being sent, one at a time.

Clock — pulses once per bit, telling the register "read the data pin now."

Latch — pulses once after all 8 bits are in, to update the outputs all at once (avoiding a visible ripple).

// shiftOut() handles the bit-by-bit clocking for you
digitalWrite(latchPin, LOW);
shiftOut(dataPin, clockPin, MSBFIRST, 0b10110010);
digitalWrite(latchPin, HIGH); // outputs update here, all at once

PWM, in depth

A digital pin can only be fully on or fully off — so how does analogWrite() fade an LED smoothly? It cheats, very fast, and your eyes fall for it.

The trick: switch fast enough that it blurs together

PWM (pulse-width modulation) flips the pin on and off roughly 500–1000 times per second. What varies is the duty cycle — the percentage of each cycle spent HIGH versus LOW. A 25% duty cycle looks noticeably dimmer than 75%, even though both are technically just on/off switching, because your eye (or a motor, or a speaker) averages it out.

analogWrite(ledPin, 128);

What the wave actually looks like

analogWrite() takes a value 0–255 — not volts, not a percentage, just where you land on that 0–255 scale (0 = always off, 255 = always on). Only certain pins support it, marked with a ~ on the board silkscreen.

PWM isn't just for LEDs. The exact same principle drives DC motor speed control and generates the control pulses that servos read as a target angle.

Interrupts

Polling — repeatedly checking "has this happened yet?" in loop() — works fine until loop() gets busy doing something slow. Interrupts are how the chip catches an event it would otherwise miss entirely.

The problem with polling

If your code is in the middle of a 2-second delay() (or any long-running operation) when a button gets pressed, digitalRead() never gets a chance to notice — the press happened while the chip's attention was elsewhere. Polling only sees what's true right now, at the exact instant it checks.

Attaching an interrupt

attachInterrupt() tells the chip: "no matter what you're doing, if this pin changes state, pause and run this function immediately." That function is called an ISR (interrupt service routine).

volatile bool buttonPressed = false; // volatile: can change outside normal flow
 
void setup() {
  pinMode(2, INPUT_PULLUP);
  attachInterrupt(digitalPinToInterrupt(2), onButtonPress, FALLING);
  Serial.begin(9600);
}
 
void onButtonPress() {
  buttonPressed = true; // keep ISRs short — just set a flag
}
 
void loop() {
  if (buttonPressed) {
    Serial.println("Caught it, even during a delay!");
    buttonPressed = false;
  }
  // ... rest of loop can still take as long as it needs
}
!

ISRs should be short. They interrupt everything else, including other interrupts — a slow ISR (or one with its own delay()) can make the whole chip feel unresponsive. Set a flag and let loop() handle the real work.

Capstone: a traffic light state machine

Everything in this track converges here. A finite state machine (FSM) is just a system that's always in exactly one named state, with defined rules for what triggers a move to the next one — and it's how real embedded firmware is structured.

The states, and the rule between them

A traffic light only needs three states, and the transitions only depend on time in this simple version (a real intersection also reacts to sensors, which is a natural next step once you're comfortable here).

The full sketch

Notice there's no delay() anywhere — a real traffic controller can't freeze the whole chip for 4 seconds while also watching a pedestrian button. It uses the same millis() timing pattern from the debouncing lesson, just driving a state variable instead of a button flag.

enum LightState { RED, GREEN, YELLOW };
LightState currentState = RED;
unsigned long stateStartTime = 0;
 
const unsigned long RED_MS = 4000;
const unsigned long GREEN_MS = 3000;
const unsigned long YELLOW_MS = 1000;
 
void setup() {
  pinMode(REDPIN, OUTPUT); pinMode(YELLOWPIN, OUTPUT); pinMode(GREENPIN, OUTPUT);
  stateStartTime = millis();
}
 
void loop() {
  unsigned long elapsed = millis() - stateStartTime;
 
  switch (currentState) {
    case RED:
      if (elapsed >= RED_MS) { currentState = GREEN; stateStartTime = millis(); }
      break;
    case GREEN:
      if (elapsed >= GREEN_MS) { currentState = YELLOW; stateStartTime = millis(); }
      break;
    case YELLOW:
      if (elapsed >= YELLOW_MS) { currentState = RED; stateStartTime = millis(); }
      break;
  }
 
  digitalWrite(REDPIN, currentState == RED);
  digitalWrite(YELLOWPIN, currentState == YELLOW);
  digitalWrite(GREENPIN, currentState == GREEN);
}
